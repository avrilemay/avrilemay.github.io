About

Introduction

Hi, I'm Avrile, a PhD Track Master student in Data & AI at Institut Polytechnique de Paris (École Polytechnique and Télécom Paris).

I work on mechanistic interpretability, AI fairness, and applied NLP, including legal NLP. I'm drawn to questions that cut across these: how social and cultural biases get encoded as circuits inside language models, how we evaluate fairness in practice, and how NLP applies to specialised domains like law.

Four research lines this year at IP Paris: legal NLP (statutory citation in French civil law), mechanistic interpretability of LLMs (cultural binding circuits), multi-agent systems (collective influence), and graph modeling of jurisprudential corpora.

  • Background: law (LL.M., Duke; Master's, Paris-Dauphine), then linguistics and teaching, now AI/NLP
  • I read and write in French and English, and speak Spanish
  • Outside the lab: PADI Divemaster, blackwater and pelagic diving

Influencer or Observer: Predicting Social Roles on Twitter

1st of 87 · 88.0% accuracy

Winning entry for the INF554 Deep Learning Kaggle challenge at École Polytechnique, with Falguny Barua Ema and Saurabh Mishra. A stacking ensemble that predicts whether a Twitter user is an influencer or an observer: LightGBM over 1,373 user-level behavioral features, fine-tuned CamemBERTa-v2 reading multi-view "user cards" of aggregated tweets, and a HistGradientBoosting meta-learner combining both signals. Behavioral and linguistic features turned out to be complementary, which is what pushed the ensemble past either model alone.

Mechanistic Interpretability

Cultural Binding Heads in Language Models

Student Researcher · SAMOVAR, Télécom SudParis
Feb 2026–Present

Why do LLMs default to equal treatment across cultural groups even when context warrants differentiation? The project asks whether cultural binding is encoded in knowledge representations or in the routing mechanism that selects which knowledge to attend to.

Method

  • Causal intervention across eight LLMs (Mistral, Llama, Gemma, Mistral-Nemo, both instruct and base)
  • Edge knockout to isolate causal mediators
  • Dose–response analysis to characterise circuit strength
  • Counterfactual steering for evaluation

Key findings

  • A small set of mid-layer attention heads causally mediates cultural binding
  • The bottleneck is routing, not knowledge
  • Identified circuits transfer from instruct to base models
  • One model family shows SFT reprogramming the binding circuit rather than amplifying it

Legal NLP · Lead Author

Where Experts Disagree, Models Fail

Student Researcher · DIG Team, LTCI, Télécom Paris
Apr 2025–Present

A benchmark for detecting implicit statutory citations (references to legal articles that judges use without quoting them) in French civil law decisions. The core finding is methodological: annotator disagreement is not noise but the central signal.

What I built

  • 1,015 passage–article pairs annotated by three legal experts (Cohen's κ = 0.33)
  • Full pipeline: Judilibre data collection, JuriBERT bi-encoder training, adversarial filtering
  • Coordination of the expert annotation campaign

Key finding

  • Annotator disagreement is the strongest predictor of model failure
  • 68% of false positives concentrate on the 33% of disputed cases

Multi-Agent Systems

Collective Nudging that Scales

Student Researcher · ACES Team, LTCI, Télécom Paris
Mar 2026–Present

A NetLogo agent-based study of how the number of shepherd dogs required to herd a flock scales with flock size. The real question behind the dogs-and-sheep framing: how does collective influence work when a small population tries to nudge a much larger one toward a goal?

Contribution

  • Proposes a local sheep-density gradient as a tractable approximation of the flock's global centre of mass
  • Characterises a viable influence range via Dmin/Dmax bounds
  • 11,000+ simulation runs sweeping over flock size, dog count, and behavioural parameters
